------------------------------------------------------------
:: The Visitor
------------------------------------------------------------

Her name is Caterina, and she is seventeen years old, and
she has come to the Istituto delle Scienze because her
brother said girls weren't allowed in. That isn't quite
true. Girls aren't encouraged. There is a difference, her
brother said, and Caterina, who has been told all her life
about differences, decided she would like to see one for
herself.

It is a Tuesday in October. The light in Bologna at this
hour is the colour of weak tea. She has worn her good shoes,
which pinch, and her second-best dress, because she did not
want to look as though she had tried.

The Palazzo Poggi is enormous. The wide stone staircase
takes her up past rooms full of glass cases — birds with
their feathers gone matte, a whale's jawbone propped in a
corner, jars in which pale things float with their eyes
half-open. She walks through them slowly. She had thought
she would feel small. Instead she feels, oddly, taller.

On the upper floor she takes a wrong turning. The corridor
narrows. The cases give way to closed doors, and the closed
doors give way to one door slightly ajar, with a thin warm
light coming from underneath it, and the smell — quite
unmistakably, in this dry old building — of fresh coffee.

She pushes the door open.

The room is not large. It is full, in the way that a head
is full: too many things, all of them important to someone.
Charts of a great river have been pinned across one wall.
On the desk: jars of cloudy water, each with a small label.
Sketches of cannon, drawn in a careful brown ink. A glass
tube, slender as a finger, lying on a velvet cloth as
though asleep. And, on the corner of the desk, a porcelain
cup the size of half an egg, still steaming.

Behind the desk, an old man.

He looks up at her. His eyes are very bright, and very
pale, like water in a shallow dish. He does not seem
surprised.

"Ah," he says. "Another one."

Caterina, who had a sentence ready, finds that she has
forgotten it.

"I — I'm sorry. I took the wrong corridor. I'll go."

"Don't." He waves a hand at the chair opposite him. The
hand is thin and freckled and, she notices, ink-stained at
the knuckle. "Most of them stay downstairs and stare at the
cases. You came up. Sit down."

She sits, because she can think of no good reason not to.

"My name is Luigi Ferdinando Marsili," the old man says,
"and this house, and most of what is in it, is mine. Or
was. I am no longer sure which. I am very old, you see, and
the line between what one has and what one has given away
becomes — blurred."

"Conte Marsili." She says it carefully, as though the
syllables are a piece of china she has been handed.

"Just Marsili will do. The title was given to me at birth.
I did nothing for it. The only things worth being called by
are the things one earns."

Caterina folds her hands in her lap. She is aware, suddenly,
of the porcelain cup, the steam still rising. He sees her
seeing it.

"Coffee," he says. "Would you like some?"

"I — I've never drunk it."

He smiles for the first time. It changes his face entirely;
he looks, for a moment, like a much younger man pretending
to be old. "Then today is a good day. Sit. We have time. I
will tell you anything you wish to know. Most visitors don't
ask. They tiptoe past the cases and tell their friends, at
dinner, that they have been to a museum. As if a museum were
a thing to be visited. A museum is a conversation. Nobody
ever wants to have it."

"What — what would I ask?"

He spreads his ink-stained hand over the clutter of his
desk. "Anything here," he says. "Anything you can see. Each
of these things is a chapter. Pick one. Pick the one that
makes you most curious. We will start there."

Caterina looks. The river charts. The cloudy jars. The
cannon. The tube on the velvet. The little cup.

What does she ask him about?

------------------------------------------------------------
:: I. The Boy in Bologna
------------------------------------------------------------

The old man closes his eyes for a moment, as though
listening for something. Then he opens them.

"My name. Yes. That is a sensible place to start, because
most things start with a name, even if they do not stay
there.

"I was born in this city, in a room not three streets from
where we are sitting, on the tenth of July, 1658. My
father was Carlo Marsili. He was a count, as his father had
been, and his father, and so on backwards through a long
line of men who had all looked, I am told, more or less
like me. My mother was Margherita Hercolani. She was also
of an old house. In Bologna we do that — we marry our
histories together, as if that will keep them safe.

"So I was a count from the moment I drew breath. I had
done nothing for it. I had merely arrived. I think this is
why titles have always meant so little to me. A thing one
has not earned is, in the end, only a thing one is wearing."

He pauses. He looks at his ink-stained knuckle.

"You will laugh at this," he says. "I never enrolled at our
university. The oldest university in the world, and it is
one street from my front door. My family expected it. The
city expected it. I — I could not bear it."

"Why not?" says Caterina.

"Because they wanted me to read what other men had already
read, and write down what other men had already written,
and call this learning. Caterina — that is your name? You
told me as you sat down — Caterina. I wanted to look at the
world, not at books about the world. There is a difference."

She thinks about this.

"My brother says the difference is that books don't get
you wet."

The old man laughs. It is a dry, surprised sound, as though
he has not laughed in a while and the machinery is rusty.

"Your brother is wrong, but charmingly so. I went to a few
lectures, you understand. I sat at the back, like a thief.
I heard Malpighi speak of the lungs, and Montanari of the
moons of Jupiter, and Trionfetti of the names of plants. I
sat very quietly and I took no notes, because I did not
want it to feel like school. I took no degree. I never
taught."

"And yet —"

"And yet." His pale eyes warm. "Half a century later, in
London, in a hall full of the cleverest men in England, Sir
Isaac Newton himself stood up to introduce me to the Royal
Society. He used the word eminent. I remember it because I
had to lower my head, so that no one would see my face. I
was thinking of a boy at the back of a Bologna lecture-hall,
who had been afraid of being asked a question. And I was
trying very hard," says the old man, "not to laugh."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: II. Two Currents
------------------------------------------------------------

"I was twenty-one," says the old man, "and the world was
the wrong size."

He takes a sip of his coffee. The little cup looks absurd
in his hand, like a thimble.

"Bologna was small. My father was small. My future, as
laid out for me, was small. So when, in 1679, the Venetian
ambassador happened to need an obliging young man to travel
in his train, I — well. I obliged."

"Where to?"

"Constantinople."

He says the word the way someone else might say a woman's
name.

"I lived there for many months. I learned a little Turkish.
I sat in coffee-houses where men older than my grandfather
played chess for hours and never raised their voices. I met
a scholar named Husayn Efendi, who had written a treatise
about coffee, of all things — coffee — and who gave me a
copy because I asked the right questions. I have his words
still. He has been dead a long time, but I read his words
sometimes, and he is not entirely dead."

Caterina is leaning forward without having decided to.

"But the city was not what gripped me," says the old man.
"The strait was what gripped me. The Bosporus."

"The water between."

"The water between. Caterina, you must understand — there
is something that happens in the Bosporus that should not
happen. There are two currents. The surface flows one way,
toward the south, into the Sea of Marmara. And underneath
it — underneath it, somehow — a deeper water flows the
other way. North. Into the Black Sea. The fishermen knew
this. They had known it for a thousand years. They felt it
when they let their nets down and the deep nets dragged
opposite to the shallow ones."

"How is that possible?"

"That is the question I asked. And nobody had an answer.
Not a real one. They had stories. They had — opinions. But
nobody, in all those centuries, had thought to lower a
bottle into the water and weigh what came back up."

He spreads his thin hands.

"So I lowered a bottle. And I weighed what came back up. I
took the surface water, and I took the deeper water, and I
compared them. The deep water was heavier. Saltier. Colder.
And from this — from this small fact, drawn up in a glass
bottle in 1679 — I began to understand something. The sea
is not one thing. It is many things, layered. It moves in
currents we cannot see. It is, in its way, alive."

"And nobody had done that before?"

"Not in that manner. Not with notes, and dates, and the
exact place written down beside each measurement. I did
not know, when I was twenty-one, that I was making the first
oceanographic measurements in the history of the world. I
only knew," he says, "that nobody else seemed willing to
get their sleeves wet."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: III. The Rába
------------------------------------------------------------

The old man is quiet for a long moment.

"This one I tell badly," he says. "I have told it many
times, and each time I tell it badly. You will forgive me."

"Of course."

"In 1681 I joined the army of the Holy Roman Emperor,
Leopold I. Why? I have given different answers in different
years. The truthful one is that I wanted to see the
borderlands — the long jagged seam where Christendom and
the Ottoman Empire ground against each other like two
millstones. A library could not show it to me. A man with
a sword and a horse could go where a library could not."

"And in 1683 —"

"In 1683 the Sultan Mehmed IV marched on Vienna with an
army the size of a small kingdom. I was not, you understand,
in the great siege. I would like to say I was. It would
make a better story. But the truth is I was in a skirmish.
A small thing. A footnote. On the banks of the river Rába,
in Hungary, in the days before the Ottoman host closed
around Vienna."

He looks at the back of his hand, where a long thin scar
runs from the wrist toward the knuckle.

"I remember a horse. I remember a noise that I thought, at
the time, was thunder, but which was a man shouting in
Turkish very close to my ear. I remember the dust. There is
always dust, on a battlefield — they don't tell you that, in
the songs. There is more dust than blood. I remember the
weight of the horse going down on top of me, and the
sudden cold of the river water against my side, because I
had fallen partly in. And I remember thinking, with a
strange detachment, So this is how it ends. I was twenty-
five years old."

"But it didn't end."

"No." The faintest smile. "It did not. Because the men who
found me were not infantry. They were Crimean Tartars,
riders, fighting for the Sultan. And they did not kill me,
which surprised me very much."

"Why didn't they?"

"Because they could see, even covered in mud, that I was a
gentleman. And a gentleman, to a Tartar in 1683, is not a
corpse. A gentleman is a coin yet to be spent."

He raises his cup. His hand is steady. Caterina notices, in
a way she will remember years later, that the steam is
nearly gone now.

"I lay on the riverbank, and the Tartar bent over me, and
he took my chin in his hand to look at my face — not
unkindly, you understand. With assessment. The way you
might look at a piece of cloth in a market, to see whether
the colour is true.

"And in that moment," he says, "I stopped being Conte
Marsili of Bologna. I became merchandise."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: IV. The Coin Yet to Be Spent
------------------------------------------------------------

"It is a small word, sold," the old man says. "Written
down, it is nothing. A few letters. But said aloud, by the
person it has happened to —"

He does not finish the sentence.

"The Tartars did not keep me," he says, after a moment.
"They could not. A wounded Italian on horseback only slows
a raiding band, and besides, they had no way to ransom me
through their own people. So they took me to where men
with money were buying.

"You should picture it, Caterina, because most people do
not. There was no auction-block. No theatre of it. It was
simply — a transaction. In the back of a tent. A man came
in, looked me over, said something I could not understand,
and money changed hands. I was carried out. I was
delivered, like a basket of bread, to another tent. Where
another man looked me over, and bought me from the first.

"In this way, link by link, I passed along the chain of the
Ottoman war-camp. I do not know how many hands I went
through. Three. Four. They blur. I had a fever for some of
it. My side was infected. There was a woman — I never
learned her name — who washed the wound with hot water and
wine, and saved my life, and never said a word to me. I
think of her sometimes. I hope she had a good death."

He stops. Caterina realises she is holding her breath.

"And at the end of the chain," he says, "I came into the
household of the Pasha of Temesvar. He looked me over the
way a man looks over a draught animal at market. He
prodded my arm. He turned my chin from side to side. He
said something to one of his servants, and the servant
laughed. I did not know, then, what he had said. Years
later, I would learn it. He had said: This one will live.

"And he was right. I did. I lived."

The old man takes another sip of his coffee. His hand,
Caterina notices, is shaking, very slightly. He sees her
seeing it, and steadies it on the desk.

"You have to understand," he says, more quietly. "The body
remembers, even when the mind has filed it away. I am an
old man now. I have been free for nearly fifty years. And
still, sometimes, when a stranger touches my arm without
warning — I — I am back in that tent. For a moment. Then I
come back to myself. But for the moment, I am there."

Caterina, very carefully, says nothing. She feels, somehow,
that this is the right thing to do.

"You are a good listener," the old man says. "Your brother
is wrong about you. Whatever it is he says about you. He is
wrong."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: V. Invisible Things
------------------------------------------------------------

"The Pasha of Temesvar," says the old man, "made me a
servant. And I think I should tell you what that meant,
because the word is not enough."

He puts the little cup down.

"I had been served, all my life. I had had a man who put on
my boots in the morning. I had had a woman who brought hot
water for my shaving. I had had a cook, and a cook's boy,
and a stable-hand whose name I never bothered to learn — I
am not proud of that, by the way. I tell you so that you
understand the ground I started from.

"And then, suddenly, I was on the other side of all of it.
I was the man who put on the boots. I was the woman who
brought the water. I was the boy in the stable. I fetched. I
carried. I poured. I emptied — I emptied things I would
rather not name to a young woman in good shoes."

Caterina, despite everything, smiles. She has not realised
until now that she likes him.

"The pasha beat me," he says, in the same level tone.
"Sometimes for cause and sometimes, I think, simply because
the day was long, and his back hurt, and there I was. I
will tell you something strange. I am not, even now, very
angry about it. I have known men who beat their dogs with
worse purpose."

"That's terrible," Caterina says.

"It is. But it is also the truth, and I have lived too long
to lie about the truth in order to seem braver than I was.

"Now. Listen. Here is the thing I want to tell you. Those
months in the pasha's tent taught me more about the
Ottoman world than any embassy could have taught any
ambassador. Because diplomats see the throne. The throne is
where they are seated when they arrive. But servants —
servants see everything else. The drill of the soldiers in
the morning, before the sun is properly up. The arrival of
the gunpowder wagons. The way the officers quarrel when
they think no one is listening. What the bey eats. What he
reads. What he is afraid of, late at night, when he calls
for a candle.

"A man who pours coffee for a pasha is invisible. The
pasha forgets him within a heartbeat of taking the cup. The
pasha will speak in front of him about troop movements, and
about a wife he loves more than the other one, and about a
debt he owes to a Greek in Salonika, as if the man pouring
the coffee were a piece of furniture.

"And the man pouring the coffee," says the old man, very
softly, "is listening to every word."

He looks at Caterina. His pale eyes have something almost
mischievous in them.

"There is a lesson in it," he says. "If you are ever in a
room where powerful men are speaking — be quiet. Be useful.
Pour the wine. Smile when smiled at. Say nothing. You will
learn more in an hour than they will learn from you in a
lifetime."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: VI. The Brothers
------------------------------------------------------------

"There came a day," the old man says, "when the pasha's
people sold me a second time. And this is the strangest
part of the story, because I think you will not believe
me."

"Try me."

He laughs his rusty laugh.

"My new owners were two brothers. They were Bosnians.
Merchants. They travelled with the army the way merchants
always travel with armies — selling and buying, sometimes
both, every commodity an army needs and several an army is
not supposed to want. They had drunk my coffee in the
pasha's tent. Many men had drunk my coffee in the pasha's
tent. But these two brothers — I do not know how to say
this without sounding as though I am inventing it — these
two brothers liked me.

"They liked the way I made the cavè. They liked, also, the
strange Italian who made it. They had questions. Where was
I from? What did Bologna look like? Was it true that there
were houses there with leaning towers? Did Christians
really worship paintings? They asked the way curious men
ask, without scorn, simply wanting to know. And I answered,
as well as I could, in my poor Turkish, and they laughed
sometimes, kindly, at my mistakes."

"What were their names?"

The old man thinks for a moment. His face changes — it goes
inward, the way a face goes inward when it is searching for
something a long way back.

"I have forgotten," he says, finally. "I am ashamed to tell
you so. I knew them once. I have written about them in my
treatise on coffee. But the names themselves — the sound of
them, the way I called out to them across a camp — those
have gone. I am old, Caterina. The names are the first
thing to go.

"What I remember is this. One morning a few coins changed
hands in the pasha's outer tent, and I belonged to them.
The taller brother put his hand on my shoulder — not
heavily, you understand, but as one places a hand on
something one has just bought — and said something to me in
his own language. I think it meant: Do not be afraid. We
will not be cruel.

"And they were not cruel. Caterina, listen. I was twice a
slave. But the Bosnian brothers treated me almost as a man.
They needed someone who could read and write, and I could
read and write in five tongues. They needed someone who
understood Christian customs, for their trade across the
lines, and I understood them. They needed someone to make
the coffee, of course — that, above all. But they needed me,
and they did not pretend otherwise, and there is a kind of
dignity in being needed honestly that I had not expected to
find in my captivity.

"I will tell you the last thing. In my own treatise on
coffee — the one I would write later, when I was free — I
wrote it down for anyone who cared to read it. I said that
it was the coffee that gave me my life. Through the making
of it, I came to know those Bosnians. And through them, I
survived.

"I owe a debt," he says, "to a bean. And to two men whose
names I have forgotten."

He looks at Caterina.

"Don't forget the names of people who are kind to you," he
says. "Write them down. I should have written them down."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: VII. A Small Cup
------------------------------------------------------------

The old man pushes the little porcelain cup across the
desk towards her.

"Drink," he says. "Don't look so frightened. It is a drink,
not a sacrament."

Caterina lifts the cup. It is lighter than she expected. The
liquid inside is the colour of wet earth, and the smell is
extraordinary — bitter and warm and somehow far away, like
a fire seen on the other side of a valley.

She takes the smallest possible sip.

"Oh," she says.

"There," says the old man, pleased. "Now you have done a
thing your grandmother never did. Welcome to the eighteenth
century."

She drinks again. It is bitter and the bitterness is — not
unpleasant. There is a sweetness underneath that she had not
expected.

"I learned to make this," he says, "in a tent that smelled
of smoke and unwashed men. The pasha gave me a charcoal
brazier, a long-handled copper pot, and a sack of dark, oily
beans. He gave me, you might say, a profession. I was now,
officially, a kahveci. A coffee-maker. As the Ottoman army
moved on Vienna, my job was to make this drink in such
quantities that an entire army could have a cup of it before
they marched.

"I learned the art slowly. To roast the bean until it
glistened with its own oil. To grind it finer than flour —
finer than you can grind anything in a Bolognese kitchen,
believe me, I have tried. The right proportion: about a
tenth part of the water. To bring the pot three times to
the boil, but never to let it overflow, because if it
overflows the foam is lost, and the foam — the foam is the
soul of the drink. The Turks have a saying about it. A
coffee without foam is like a wedding without music."

"Did you make it for the soldiers?"

"For the soldiers. For the officers. For the pasha himself,
in his innermost tent, and for the Bosnian brothers later,
when they came to own me. I made it in such quantities,
Caterina, that my tent became — these are my own words,
which I wrote down later — like one of our Italian inns. A
coffee-shop made of canvas. Soldiers came in, drank, paid
nothing, went out again. I poured. I poured. I poured.

"And while I poured, I gathered. From my own observation.
From the talk of the soldiers — soldiers tell their
coffee-maker things they would never tell their wives. From
the writings of Husayn Efendi, the Ottoman scholar I had
met in Constantinople years before. I asked: What is this
drink? Where does the plant grow? What does it do to the
body? Why does the East worship it, and the West, until
now, ignore it?

"After my ransom, in 1685, I took my notes to Vienna and
published them. A short book. I gave it a long Italian
title, because that was the fashion, and the title ended in
the words historia medica del cavè — the medical history of
coffee."

He looks at the cup in her hand.

"It was among the very first books on the drink ever
printed in Europe," he says. "Caterina — drink that. Don't
let it go cold. A captive, you might say, gave his
captors' beverage to his own continent. And here we are,
forty-five years later, in Bologna, in October, with you
sitting across from me drinking it as though it had always
been here.

"I am rather proud of that," he says, "even now."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: VIII. The Dangerous Pages
------------------------------------------------------------

The old man's face changes. The lightness goes out of it. He
glances, almost involuntarily, at the closed door behind
Caterina, as though even now, in his own house, half a
century later, he is not quite sure he is alone.

"There was another book," he says, "that I wrote during my
captivity. And it was a more dangerous one. I have not
talked about it as often. I will talk about it now."

He folds his hands on the desk.

"I made the coffee, yes. I poured it for the pasha. I
poured it for the officers who came and went. I poured it
for the Bosnian brothers, when they came to own me, and for
their guests, who were sometimes other merchants and
sometimes — sometimes men whose business was less obvious. I
listened to all of them.

"And in the hours when no one was watching — at night,
mostly, by the light of a stub of candle stolen from the
pasha's stores — I wrote.

"I wrote down what I had seen. The structure of the Ottoman
army. How the regiments were organised, and named, and paid.
How the camps were laid out — where the powder was kept,
where the horse-lines were, how the watches were posted.
How the orders moved through the chain of command, and how
long they took to move, and where they could be intercepted.
I wrote of the janissaries, and the sipahis, and the
irregulars who fought on the flanks for booty. I wrote of
the way they ate, and the way they prayed, and the way they
buried their dead.

"And I wrote, above all, of the cannons."

"The cannons."

"The great cannons the Sultan had brought against Vienna. I
do not think you can imagine them, Caterina. They were the
size of carriages. It took twenty oxen to draw one along a
road. They were made in Constantinople, in foundries beside
the sea, by men whose grandfathers had been gun-makers
before them. I wrote down their dimensions. Their bore.
Their carriages. The way the gunners served them — the
order in which a man rammed, and a man primed, and a man
fired. I drew them. I drew them as carefully as I have ever
drawn anything in my life, including the engravings in my
book about the Danube. And I hid the drawings inside a
roll of laundry."

Caterina is staring at him.

"You have to understand," he says, "what those pages were
worth. Nobody in Christendom truly knew these things.
Ambassadors saw parades. Spies saw fragments. I lived
inside the camp for months, with my hands free and my
mouth shut. I was the best-informed slave the Habsburg
Emperor could have wished for, and he did not even know
yet that I would be his again."

"What would have happened, if they had found the pages?"

The old man looks at her. His pale eyes are very steady.

"They would have killed me," he says. "Not quickly. I do
not say this to alarm you. I say it because it is true,
and because you asked. They would have killed me very
slowly, in front of the entire camp, as a lesson. I knew
this every night, when I lay down in the corner of the
tent with the laundry beside me. I knew this every morning,
when I woke up and counted the laundry to make sure it had
not been disturbed. For eight months I knew this.

"And I wrote anyway."

He shakes his head.

"I cannot tell you why. I was twenty-five. I was a fool. I
was, perhaps, very angry — angrier than I admitted to
myself at the time. I think now that the writing was the
thing that kept me alive. Not the coffee. The writing. To
write down what was being done to me, and to those around
me, was to refuse to be only a servant. Do you understand?
A man with a manuscript hidden in his laundry is a
different man from a man without one. He is still pouring
the coffee. But he is not, any longer, only pouring the
coffee."

He is quiet for a moment.

"The manuscript I began in the pasha's tent," he says,
"became the foundation of a book that was published in
Amsterdam, in 1732 — two years after my death, you
understand, so I am speaking to you now of a book I have
not yet seen — under the title L'État militaire de l'empire
ottoman. The Military State of the Ottoman Empire. A whole
army, anatomised by a man who had served it coffee."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: IX. The Long Way Home
------------------------------------------------------------

"In the spring of 1684," says the old man, "the ransom
was at last arranged. Eight months after I was taken, I
walked out of the Ottoman camp a free man.

"I will tell you the truth about that walk, because nobody
ever asks. I had imagined it for so long, every night,
lying in the corner of the tent. I had imagined the
moment I would step past the last sentry. I had imagined
running. I had imagined weeping. I had imagined falling on
my knees in the dirt and kissing it.

"And what actually happened was — nothing. I walked. I
walked past a sentry who did not look at me. I walked down
a path. I walked across a field. I walked, in fact, for
about an hour, because there was nowhere immediately to go,
and the men who had come to receive me had set up their
camp at a sensible distance. And the whole time I walked, I
felt — empty. Not happy. Not free. Empty. As though I had
left something important behind in the tent and could not
remember what."

"What had you left behind?"

The old man looks at her with something like surprise.

"Nobody has ever asked me that," he says. "Caterina, I
think the answer is — I had left behind the man I had been
before the capture. He was still there, in the tent. He
did not come out with me. The man who came out was a
different man. It took me years to understand this. Some
days I am still understanding it."

He clears his throat.

"You will think I went home, of course. You will think I
returned to Bologna and lived quietly with my title and my
palazzo and considered myself fortunate to be alive."

"And you didn't."

"I did not. I went back to the war."

"Why?"

He spreads his hands.

"Because I was now the only Christian officer in Europe who
had lived inside the enemy's tents. My knowledge was worth
a regiment. And because — because I did not know how to be
quiet yet. The quiet would come later. The quiet would
come here, in this room, when I was old. But in 1686 I was
twenty-eight years old, and I had a manuscript in my baggage
about the Ottoman army, and I had the cup of cavè still on
my tongue, and I had — Caterina — I had a fury I did not
know how to set down.

"So I rejoined the army of Leopoldo. We retook Buda in
1686. I knew, from inside, how the Ottoman walls were
built, and how their guns were placed, and where the
weaknesses were. After Buda came Belgrade. After Belgrade,
a dozen lesser sieges. And in 1699, the Treaty of Karlowitz,
which I helped to negotiate. I was sent as the Emperor's
plenipotentiary to draw the new border between two empires
across hundreds of miles of forest and river. I was thirty-
nine years old, and I was a general."

"A general."

"A general. The slave who had poured the coffee, ended his
soldiering as a general of the Empire. There is a kind of
poetry in it, if you have the stomach for that kind of
poetry. I do not always have the stomach for it. Sometimes
I think — what would have happened, if the laundry had been
disturbed? Who would I have been?

"And the answer," he says, "is that I would not have been.
At all."

He is quiet for a long moment.

"That is enough soldiering for today," he says. "Tell me.
What is your brother like?"

"My brother?"

"You mentioned him."

"Oh. He — he is my older brother. He is studying law. He
told me girls weren't allowed in the Institute."

"And here you are."

"Here I am."

The old man smiles. "Good," he says. "When you next see
him, tell him you drank coffee with a count who told you
about his time as a slave, and ask him whether he has done
anything as interesting this week."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: X. The River, Drawn
------------------------------------------------------------

"The border I drew at Karlowitz," says the old man,
gesturing at the great chart pinned to the wall behind him,
"followed, for much of its length, a great grey-green
serpent of water. The Danube."

Caterina turns to look. The chart is enormous. It shows a
river that twists and folds back on itself, with little
sketched towns along its banks, and birds in the margins,
and tiny ships drawn in passing.

"Most men, asked to draw a line on a map, would have drawn
it and gone home. I spent years on the river. Years."

"What were you doing?"

"Everything. Caterina, the question is what was I not
doing. The Emperor required me to be an engineer — to
measure, and chart, and survey, and certify a frontier in
ink that no one would dispute, so that two empires could
agree about where one of them ended and the other began.
That was my official task. I did it. I did it well.

"But I was on a river. Do you understand? I was on a river,
with a boat, and an assistant, and a great deal of paper,
and nobody breathing down my neck. And I could not, by my
nature, look at a thing without asking what it was made of
and how it worked."

He counts on his fingers.

"I measured the Danube's width, and its depth, and the
speed of its current at every point I could reach. I climbed
its banks to copy Roman inscriptions older than this city.
I dragged nets through its waters and drew every fish I
caught — and I caught fish nobody had ever drawn before, and
some of them I named. I sketched the birds that nested in
its reeds. I sketched their eggs. Caterina — I do not think
anyone, before me, had thought to draw the eggs."

"Why the eggs?"

"Because the eggs are the beginning of the bird. And if you
do not understand the beginning of a thing, you do not
understand the thing. I cracked the rocks of its valleys
and named their minerals. I surveyed the ruins of fortresses
no European had described in a century. I noted the plants.
I noted the insects. There is a beetle that lives on the
banks of the Danube near Belgrade — I do not know its
modern name — that I described with such care that I am
told it is still my description scholars use, all these
years later. That beetle is, in some small way, my beetle.
It pleases me to know it.

"All of it," he says, "went into one work. My great work."

He stands up — slowly, painfully — and crosses the room to
a tall bookshelf. From the lowest shelf he draws out a
volume so heavy he has to use both hands. He places it on
the desk between them. The leather is the colour of dried
blood.

"DANUBIUS PANNONICO-MYSICUS," he says. "Six volumes,
folio-sized. The engravings — Caterina, look at the
engravings — were made by the finest hands in Amsterdam.
Published, at last, at The Hague in 1726. By then I was
already old, and the river had been finished with me for
years. But here it is."

He opens the volume. Caterina leans forward. The page shows
a fish — a great pale fish with a long, almost human-
looking face, drawn with such precision she can count the
scales along its back.

"It is, I am told," says the old man, "one of the most
extraordinary opera of its age. An entire river, between
covers. I do not say so to boast. I say so because I want
you to understand. It began as the field-notes of an
officer drawing a line. And it became — because I could
not help myself — an encyclopaedia."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: XI. The Ampoule
------------------------------------------------------------

"Come closer," says the old man, very gently. "I want to
show you something."

Caterina rises and comes around the desk. The old man lifts
the slender glass tube from its bed of velvet. He holds it
on the flat of his palm, the way another man might hold a
small bird.

"Look at it," he says. "A glass tube, weighted at one end.
Markings along the side. A stopper at the top. It does not
look like much, does it?"

"No."

"It is not much. That is the point. The most useful tools
in this world are the simple ones. I designed this — well,
the original of this — when I was twenty-one, on the
Bosporus. I have used variations of it for the rest of my
life."

"What does it do?"

"It measures water." He places the tube, very carefully,
into one of the cloudy jars on the desk. The tube floats,
upright. "Watch the level."

Caterina watches. The line of water settles against one of
the markings on the side of the glass.

"Now," he says. "If this water were saltier — denser,
heavier — the tube would float higher. If it were less
salty, it would sink lower. By reading the level on the
side, I can tell you, very precisely, the density of the
water. And by holding it correctly, and waiting, the same
instrument tells me the temperature.

"I called it an ampoule. It is a small word, ampoule. But
with it, Caterina, I did something nobody had done before.
I lowered it into the sea at known places, on known days,
and I wrote down what it told me. Not stories. Not
opinions. Numbers. Numbers attached to a place, and to a
day, and to a depth.

"The Bosporus, in 1679. The Aegean, the same year. The
Sea of Marmara. Years later, the Mediterranean off the
coast of Provence, when I was a wandering man without a
country. Each measurement is a sentence. All the
measurements together — they are a book. They are a kind of
book that no one had written before."

He lifts the tube out of the jar, and sets it back on its
velvet, with a tenderness that is almost embarrassing to
witness.

"I have lived long enough," he says, "to see other men, in
other countries, take up the practice. They will take it
further than I did. They will lower better instruments
into deeper waters. They will draw the bottom of the sea
the way we draw the surface of the moon. I will not see
that. But I will say, with the small vanity of a dying
man — the first numbers were mine.

"And every numbered list of measurements that any
oceanographer makes, anywhere in the world, for the rest of
human history — somewhere underneath it is a slender glass
tube on a velvet cloth, and an old man in Bologna who
liked to ask the sea questions."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: XII. The Wet World
------------------------------------------------------------

"In 1703," says the old man, "I lost a fortress. It is
called Breisach. You will not have heard of it, and that is
just as well. The story is not flattering. I was the
commanding officer, and I surrendered when I should not
have surrendered, and I was court-martialled, and demoted,
and stripped of my property, and disgraced.

"I do not bring it up to make you sorry for me. I bring it
up because — because it was the best thing that ever
happened to me."

"How can disgrace be the best thing?"

"Because it freed me. Caterina, listen. For twenty-two
years I had been a soldier. I had been a useful man. I had
been a man with duties. After Breisach, the Emperor had no
more use for me. And for the first time in my adult life,
I had — nothing. I had nothing to do. I had nowhere to be.
I had no orders. I had only myself, and my notebooks, and
a great deal of unspent curiosity.

"So I wandered. Switzerland, where the air is cold and
clean. Milan, briefly. The court of the Sun King, at
Versailles, where I was received with rather more ceremony
than I expected — the French enjoy a disgraced general
almost as much as they enjoy a fashionable one. And at
last, the harbour of Cassis, on the coast of Provence."

He rests his chin on his fist.

"I returned to the sea. To what I had begun as a young man
on the Bosporus. I lowered my ampoule. I drew up water
from the surface, and from the deep. I sounded the bottom,
weight on a long line, listening for the catch when it
struck. I dredged. Caterina — when you dredge, you bring
up the strangest things. Things you never imagined existed.

"I brought up corals. The men of my time believed corals
were plants. They had always believed it. The word for them,
in Latin, places them firmly among the plants. I looked at
them under a glass, and I watched them, and I came to
believe — though I could not yet prove it, and learned men
laughed at me — that corals were animals. Tiny animals,
living in great communities, building their own houses out
of stone. I was right, you know. It took fifty years for the
world to agree with me, but I was right.

"I made charts of the sea-floor as carefully as I had once
made charts of Hungarian fortresses. I drew the slope of
the bottom as it fell away from the coast of Provence. I
drew the rocks and the sand and the strange crevasses
where the depth dropped suddenly. I was a cartographer
twice over. Of the dry world, where soldiers fight. And of
the wet world, beneath, which no one had yet thought to
map."

He smiles, faintly.

"All of it went, in the end, into a book. Written in
French, because the French are good readers, and published
in Amsterdam in 1725 — Histoire physique de la mer. The
Physical History of the Sea. The first work of its kind in
any language.

"Some now tell me I should be called a founder of
oceanography. The word is ugly. I have written to one
correspondent that I find it ugly. He wrote back to say
that he agreed, but that the ugly words tend to be the
ones that stick, and I should resign myself."

He shrugs, with great delicacy.

"I have," he says, "resigned myself."


------------------------------------------------------------
:: XIII. No Monsters
------------------------------------------------------------

The old man leans forward across the desk. His eyes are
brighter now than they have been all afternoon. The light
from the window has shifted; it is the long, slow light of
the late October afternoon, gold in the dust.

"I will tell you the idea I am proudest of," he says. "It
is also the idea for which men laughed at me most. Some of
them laughed in print. I have the pamphlets in a drawer.
I keep them. I don't know why. Vanity, perhaps. Or — no.
Stubbornness. To remind myself that I was right.

"In my century, Caterina, the deep ocean is still a place
for monsters. The maps leave it blank, or they fill it with
serpents — drawn by men who have never been there, you
understand, drawn by men who have never lowered so much as
a fishhook into deep water. Sailors, even good sailors, will
tell you in all sincerity that beneath the surface lies a
separate world. Bottomless. Magical. Peopled by creatures
no fisherman has ever caught and no map has ever shown."

"And you didn't believe it."

"I did not believe it. And, Caterina, I did not believe it
not because I was clever. I did not believe it because I
had looked. I had spent twenty years lowering things into
water and pulling things up. I had walked along beaches at
low tide and watched what was uncovered. I had sounded the
bottom from a dozen boats. I had drawn what I dredged.

"And what I had seen, every time, was — land.

"Not magic. Not monsters. Land. Sand, like the sand of any
beach. Rocks, like the rocks of any hillside. Slopes, that
fell gently away the way a Tuscan field falls toward a
river. Sometimes valleys. Sometimes ridges. The same kind
of land, Caterina, that we walk upon. The same kind of
land. Continuing, beneath the water, exactly as it
continues above it.

"The continents do not stop at the shore. They only go on,
drowned."

She turns this over in her mind.

"That sounds — obvious," she says, slowly.

"It does, doesn't it?" The old man laughs, delighted. "It
sounds obvious. It is obvious. It is the most obvious thing
in the world, once you have looked. But for a thousand
years no one had looked. They had told stories. They had
filled in the blank spaces with serpents. And here came a
disgraced Italian general with a glass tube, telling them
they were wrong.

"I will tell you what I think now, in my old age. I think
the deep is not magical. The deep is geographical. Mountains
under the water are still mountains. Plains are still
plains. One day — long after my bones are dust — men will
chart the floor of the sea as carefully as you can chart
Italy. They will draw maps of it. They will give names to
its mountains. They will know which currents run where, and
which valleys are deeper than which, and which fish live in
which deeps.

"And on those maps," he says, "if anyone is generous
enough to remember, there will be a small note in a corner.
It will say: After Marsili, who first saw that the floor
of the sea was a country.

"I will not be there to read it. But you may — Caterina, if
you outlive me, and you read it one day in a book, in
fifty years, in a hundred — read it for me. Read it for
me. Will you?"

She nods. She cannot, for a moment, speak.


------------------------------------------------------------
:: XIV. The Gift
------------------------------------------------------------

The room has filled with that gold late-afternoon light.
The old man's face, in it, looks softer. Younger. As
though the light is a kind of forgiveness.

"And so we come, at last, to this room," he says. "To this
house. To the place around you."

He gestures — slowly, with that ink-stained hand — at the
walls. At the door. At the corridor beyond, with its
glass cases, and the floor below, where Caterina had walked
in not knowing what she was walking into.

"After my disgrace at Breisach I had time, for the first
time in my life, to think about what I owed. I was a
Bolognese. My city had given me a name, an education of
sorts, and a place to come back to when the world was
finished with me. I had spent forty years collecting. Books.
Instruments. Fossils. Fish in spirits. Shells. Bones.
Telescopes. Microscopes. Maps. Dried specimens of every
kingdom of nature. It was a world, Caterina, gathered into
chests. It was the pattern of forty years of an unusual
life, sorted and labelled and ready to be read.

"I gave it away."

"All of it?"

"All of it. In 1712 I made the donation to the Senate of
Bologna. Manuscripts. Instruments. Collection. Eventually
even my palazzo — the very building you are sitting in. I
had only one condition."

"What condition?"

"That they make a place. A place where young men — and, I
hope, eventually, young women — could study the natural
world directly. Not by quoting Aristotle. Not by reciting
commentaries written by other men who had read other
commentaries written by other men still. By experiment.
By observation. By lowering ampoules into water and looking
at what comes up. By cracking rocks. By sketching eggs. By
drinking coffee."

He smiles.

"I called it the Istituto delle Scienze. It opened in 1715.
Six professors took charge of its divisions. Geography.
Astronomy. Anatomy. Chemistry. Nautical studies — yes, in
landlocked Bologna I insisted upon nautical studies,
because if a thing is worth knowing, Caterina, it is worth
knowing in any city, even a city without a sea. I paid for
its observatory by sending the Pope eight little paintings
of the planets. He liked them very much. The world is
sometimes governed by such things — by little paintings.

"And then. Listen. In 1715 the Académie des Sciences in
Paris elected me. In 1722 the Royal Society in London
elected me, and Newton himself made the introduction. I
have already told you about Newton. The boy who never took
a degree, Caterina. Imagine."

He looks at her for a long moment. His pale eyes have gone
shiny. Caterina is not sure whether it is the light or
something else.

"You have been very kind," he says, "to listen to an old
man. Most visitors don't. They tiptoe through the
corridors and look at the cases and tell their friends, at
dinner, that they have been to a museum. They miss the
point. The point is not the cases."

"What is the point?"

"The point," says the old man, "is to use them. To open
them. To take the instruments out and hold them and ask
what they are for. To copy down the names on the labels and
go and find the things in the world. Do you understand me,
Caterina? The world is not a catalogue. The world is a
thing to be measured. To be roasted, ground, brewed, and
drunk. To be lowered into and pulled up out of. To be
walked on, the dry world and the wet world both, with eggs
sketched and beetles named and fortresses surveyed and
borders drawn and re-drawn."

He stands, slowly. Caterina rises with him.

"Now," he says. "It is late. The afternoon is wearing thin.
I am tired, and you have a brother to surprise. You will
go down the staircase the way you came up, and you will
walk past the cases, and you will let your hand brush along
the wood — just there, just along the top of the cases,
where the polish is worn — and you will think: this house
was built for me. Because it was. It was built, in part,
for you."

He takes her hand. His fingers are very thin and very warm.

"Come back," he says. "Come back and tell me what you have
become. I will not be here, of course. I am very old, and
this winter will probably be my last. But — come back. And
when you do, remember me. That is all an old man asks. To
be remembered, by someone who once drank coffee with him,
in October, in a room with the late light coming in."

"I will."

"Good." He releases her hand. "Now go. The cases are
waiting. The door is just there. Close it gently behind you.
The latch sticks."

She goes. At the threshold she turns to look back. The old
man is already bent again over his charts of the Danube,
his thin hand reaching, almost without thinking, for the
porcelain cup.

She closes the door gently, the way he asked.

The latch sticks. Then it gives.

In the corridor, she stands for a long moment, listening to
nothing. Then she goes down the stairs, past the cases, and
out into the cool blue evening of Bologna.

She walks slowly. She is in no hurry.

She is going to remember this for the rest of her life.
